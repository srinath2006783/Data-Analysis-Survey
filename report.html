Stress, Burnout & Work–Life Balance

Pilot Survey • Ages 16–24 • 39 respondents

Key Insights

The responses indicate that stress among individuals aged 16–24 is not evenly distributed. Instead, it clusters around specific age groups and roles, suggesting that exposure varies more by life stage and daily structure than by individual disposition.

Overall Risk Distribution

Most respondents fall into the medium-risk category, suggesting persistent, day-to-day stress rather than acute burnout. A smaller but notable portion experiences high risk, indicating sustained pressure that may require attention and support.

Green indicates low stress, yellow reflects moderate strain, and red highlights elevated risk levels.

Risk Patterns

Breaking down risk levels by age and role helps reveal where stress tends to concentrate, offering insight into how life stage and daily structure shape emotional load.

By Age Group

Individuals aged 19–21 show the highest concentration of medium and high stress, coinciding with academic transitions and identity shifts.

By Role

College students account for most elevated risk responses, while non-academic roles show comparatively lower stress exposure.

Top Stress Causes (High Risk)

Among respondents experiencing high stress, academic and workload-related pressures dominate. Time scarcity and unclassified personal factors further contribute to elevated strain.

  • Academics / Work — 4 respondents
  • Other personal factors — 3 respondents
  • Lack of time — 2 respondents

What This Suggests

Taken together, these patterns indicate that stress within this age group is less about isolated individual experiences and more about sustained exposure linked to specific life stages and roles. Medium-risk responses dominate across most groups, pointing toward ongoing pressure rather than short-term spikes.

The concentration of higher risk among college-aged respondents, alongside the absence of uniformly elevated stress across all roles, suggests that stress in this dataset is contextual rather than inevitable. Structural factors such as academic expectations and time constraints appear to shape outcomes more strongly than individual variation.
